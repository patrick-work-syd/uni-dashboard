The Fall of Persia: Conquest and Empire

ANHS2609: Alexander and the Hellenistic World

Focus: Alexander's invasion of the Persian Empire (334-331 BC), major battles (Granicus, Issus, Gaugamela), siege of Tyre, Egypt and the oracle at Siwah, military tactics, and the fall of the world's greatest superpower
Course Progress Step 1 of 10
1

The Granicus River (334 BC)

Alexander's first major battle against the Persian Empire occurred at the Granicus River in northwestern Asia Minor (modern-day Turkey). This battle was crucial as it established Alexander's credibility and began the conquest of Persian-held territory.

The Challenge:

  • Facing Persian satraps' armies determined to stop Alexander before he gained a foothold in Asia
  • Dangerous river crossing with enemy cavalry waiting on the opposite bank
  • First direct confrontation between Macedonian and Persian forces

Alexander's Strategy:

  • Bold cavalry charge across the river, personally leading from the front
  • Exploited his superior cavalry tactics and the sarissa-armed phalanx
  • Demonstrated personal courage that became his trademark
This was more than a military victory — it signaled to the Greek city-states and the Persian Empire that Alexander was a formidable threat who would not be easily stopped.
2

The Battle of Issus (333 BC)

At Issus in southern Asia Minor, Alexander confronted Darius III, the Great King of Persia himself, in person. This was the culmination of events after Granicus — Darius decided to personally lead his forces to crush Alexander.

Key Aspects:

  • The Terrain Advantage: The narrow coastal plain limited Persia's numerical superiority. Alexander's smaller army could not be outflanked on this restricted battlefield
  • Darius's Presence: The Great King's personal involvement motivated his troops but also meant his defeat would be catastrophic for Persian morale
  • Tactical Innovation: Alexander used a hammer-and-anvil tactic — the phalanx held while cavalry wheeled around the flank

The Outcome: Darius III fled the battlefield, leaving his family behind as prisoners. This was a stunning psychological victory that shattered the myth of Persian invincibility.

The famous mosaic from Pompeii depicts this moment — Alexander on his horse, Darius in his chariot, the personalization of ancient warfare between two civilizations.
3

The Siege of Tyre (332 BC)

After Issus, Alexander did not immediately pursue Darius. Instead, he turned south to eliminate Persian naval power. Tyre, the greatest naval power of the Levant, refused to submit. This led to one of the ancient world's most famous sieges.

The Problem for Alexander:

  • Tyre was an island city, naturally defended by the sea
  • Alexander's forces were primarily land-based, with inferior naval capability
  • A six-month siege would tie up his resources

Alexander's Audacious Solution:

  • Built a massive causeway from the mainland to the island (the mole of Tyre)
  • Used siege engines and engineering innovation to breach walls
  • Eventually captured and destroyed the city, making an example of resistance

Why This Mattered: Elimination of Tyre secured Alexander's rear and eliminated Persian naval bases. It also demonstrated his determination — he would not be deterred by obstacles or time constraints.

4

Alexander in Egypt & Siwah

After Tyre, Alexander marched into Egypt (332-331 BC), where he was received as a liberator from Persian rule. Rather than immediately pursuing Darius, Alexander made a detour to the oracle of Zeus Ammon in the remote oasis of Siwah.

The Journey to Siwah:

  • A grueling three-month journey across the desert to reach the oracle
  • Three months back = six months away from his main campaign
  • High risk but strategically important to Alexander

The Oracle's Message: The oracle reportedly greeted Alexander as the son of Zeus-Ammon. What exactly was said remains unclear, but the implications were profound — Alexander could now claim divine parentage, legitimizing his rule in both Greek and Egyptian eyes.

This visit raises fundamental questions: Was Alexander seeking military advice? Seeking legitimacy? Genuinely believing in his divinity? The historical sources offer different interpretations, and scholars debate his motivations.
5

The Founding of Alexandria

While in Egypt, Alexander founded the city of Alexandria, which would become one of the most important cities in the ancient world and a center of learning and culture in the Hellenistic period.

Strategic Location:

  • Founded on the Egyptian coast at the mouth of the Nile Delta
  • Positioned to serve as a major Mediterranean trading port
  • Could control both Egyptian and eastern Mediterranean commerce

Symbolic Importance:

  • A Greek city in Egypt — symbol of Hellenistic cultural fusion
  • Named after Alexander himself, ensuring his lasting fame
  • Would later house the Great Library and Museum (under the Ptolemies)

Urban Planning: Alexander planned the city's grid layout personally, showing his interest in not just conquest but in building and permanent settlement. Alexandria outlived Alexander by more than 2,000 years.

6

The Battle of Gaugamela (331 BC)

After Egypt, Alexander finally pursued Darius III into Mesopotamia. On October 1, 331 BC, they met at the plain of Gaugamela (near modern Irbil, Iraq). This was the decisive battle that ended the Persian Empire as a unified state.

The Numbers Game:

  • Darius brought a massive army — estimates range from 200,000 to 1,000,000 men (likely exaggerated by sources)
  • Alexander had roughly 47,000 troops — outnumbered but superior discipline and tactics
  • This was a David vs. Goliath moment that Alexander's forces won through skill, not numbers

Alexander's Tactical Masterpiece:

  • The phalanx formed the center, unbreakable as always
  • Alexander personally led the cavalry on the right flank in a devastating charge
  • Created a gap in the Persian line that widened into a rout

The Turning Point: Darius III fled again — this time, he would be assassinated by his own men before Alexander could capture him. The great Persian Empire, which had stood for 200 years, collapsed.

7

The Fall of Persepolis

After Gaugamela, Alexander marched to Persepolis, the ceremonial capital of the Persian Empire. The fall of this city symbolized the complete destruction of Persian imperial power.

The Physical Conquest:

  • Alexander captured Persepolis without a major siege — it was relatively lightly defended
  • The city contained the royal palace, treasury, and administrative records of the empire
  • Alexander's forces looted the treasury and then burned the palace — either deliberately or in a drunken celebration

Symbolic Destruction: The burning of Persepolis was both a practical decision (destroying the administrative center of Persian power) and a theatrical one (demonstrating total victory and the end of an era).

The Imperial Transformation: After Persepolis, Alexander began adopting Persian customs, dress, and court etiquette. He no longer saw himself as a conqueror of Persia but as its legitimate heir and Great King.

The destruction of Persepolis remains controversial — was it justified military strategy or an act of destructive hubris? Ancient sources give different accounts, and historians debate Alexander's intentions and state of mind.
8

Darius III's Fate & Persian Perspective

The story of Darius III provides insight into the human cost of Alexander's conquest and the Persian view of events that Greek sources often marginalize.

The Last Great King:

  • Darius III was not an incompetent ruler — he led armies, made strategic decisions, and commanded loyalty
  • Yet he was defeated twice by someone half his age with fewer resources
  • After Gaugamela, he fled eastward, hoping to rally forces for a third confrontation

His Death: Before Alexander could capture him, Darius III was assassinated by Bessus, one of his own satraps, near the Caspian Sea. This assassination is itself dramatic — a dying king stabbed by a man claiming to save the Persian Empire from foreign conquest.

The Persian Perspective: For Persians, Alexander's conquest was not a glorious triumph but a catastrophe. Cities burned, the imperial structure collapsed, and Greek/Macedonian rule replaced Persian dominance in the Near East. Sources like Briant (1996) emphasize this perspective that Greek sources often downplay.

9

Primary Sources: Arrian, Plutarch & Others

Our knowledge of Alexander's Persian campaign comes largely from ancient literary sources written long after events. Understanding these sources is critical for evaluating the reliability of what we know.

Key Sources:

  • Arrian's Anabasis — Written in the 2nd century AD, largely based on eyewitness accounts (lost sources by Ptolemy and Aristobulus). Most detailed and considered most reliable by scholars
  • Plutarch's Life of Alexander — Biographical, more interested in character than military detail. Contains anecdotes and psychological insight
  • Diodorus Siculus — Universal history that includes Alexander's campaigns
  • Ancient Persian sources — Limited compared to Greek sources, but some survive in later compilations

Source Evaluation: All sources were written centuries after Alexander's death, creating risks of distortion and legend. Literary sources tend to emphasize Alexander's genius and minimize the role of his commanders and accidents of chance.

The further back you trace a source, the more reliable it tends to be. Arrian based his work on near-contemporary accounts, making him our best source despite being written 400 years after Alexander.
10

Legacy & Debate: Conquest vs Diplomacy

The fall of the Persian Empire invites fundamental questions about Alexander's strategy and its long-term consequences.

The Conquest Narrative: Alexander is celebrated as the ultimate military conqueror — brilliant tactics, personal bravery, never defeated in battle. His strategies set the standard for ancient warfare.

Alternative Perspectives:

  • Total destruction: The complete annihilation of Persian power meant no stable partnership or integration. This created instability that his successors inherited
  • Darius's failure: Was Darius incompetent, or was Alexander simply a once-in-a-generation military genius? Scholars debate how much was Alexander's superiority vs. Persian weakness
  • Long-term sustainability: Alexander's empire fell apart immediately after his death, suggesting his military success did not create lasting political stability

Modern Debate: Some historians argue Alexander would have benefited from diplomacy and integration rather than total conquest. Others argue conquest was the only strategy available given the ideological distance between Macedonian and Persian culture.

Week 5 will explore what happened AFTER the Persian Empire fell — how Alexander continued east, what happened to his empire after his death, and how the Hellenistic world that emerged was built on the ruins of Persian power.

Self-Test Questions: Week 4

Test your understanding with these 12 questions covering all major concepts from this week. Answers reveal on click — attempt the question first before looking!

Q1: What was the strategic significance of the Battle of Granicus (334 BC)?
Remembering
The Granicus River was Alexander's first major battle against Persian forces in Asia. A Macedonian victory here established Alexander's credibility, gained a foothold in Persian territory, and demonstrated that his forces could defeat Persian armies — crucially important for morale and legitimacy.
Q2: Why was the Battle of Issus (333 BC) considered a greater psychological victory than a mere military one?
Understanding
At Issus, Alexander defeated Darius III, the Great King himself, in person. Darius's presence meant the defeat of the Persian Empire's highest authority. Darius fled the battlefield, shattering the myth of Persian invincibility and showing that even the greatest king of the world's superpower could be defeated by Alexander's army.
Q3: How did Alexander overcome Tyre's natural island defenses during the siege of 332 BC?
Remembering
Alexander built a massive causeway (the mole) from the mainland to the island, allowing his forces to reach Tyre's walls. He then used siege engines and engineering innovation to breach the walls. This six-month siege demonstrated his determination and engineering capabilities.
Q4: What was the significance of Alexander's visit to the oracle of Zeus Ammon at Siwah?
Analysing
The oracle reportedly hailed Alexander as the son of Zeus Ammon. This provided divine legitimacy in both Greek and Egyptian culture, supporting his claim to rule. Whether Alexander genuinely believed this or used it for propaganda remains debated, but it transformed his image from mere military conqueror to a divinely ordained ruler.
Q5: Why did Alexander deviate from his main campaign to spend six months visiting Egypt and Siwah?
Analysing
Open-ended answer. Possible motivations include: (1) securing Egypt as a critical resource base and trading center, (2) seeking legitimacy through the oracle to consolidate rule, (3) genuine religious devotion or curiosity about divinity, (4) strategic pause to ensure stability in conquered territories before pursuing Darius. Historians debate which motive dominated.
Q6: What made the Battle of Gaugamela (331 BC) the "decisive" battle despite Alexander being outnumbered?
Applying
Alexander's tactical innovation (phalanx hold + cavalry flank charge), superior discipline of his troops, personal leadership by Alexander, and the psychological impact of Darius fleeing again all contributed. But critically, Gaugamela ended Darius's ability to field another army — the Persian Empire as a political entity collapsed afterward.
Q7: What was the significance of the founding of Alexandria in 331 BC?
Understanding
Alexandria became one of the ancient world's greatest cities — a hub of Mediterranean trade, later home to the Great Library and Museum. It symbolized Hellenistic cultural fusion (Greek city in Egypt) and ensured Alexander's lasting fame through urbanization, not just conquest. It outlasted empires and became legendary.
Q8: Why was Memnon of Rhodes considered one of the greatest threats to Alexander's campaign, and what might have happened if he had survived?
Analysing
Memnon was a Greek mercenary general fighting for Persia who had spent time in Macedon and understood the Macedonian phalanx and Alexander's critical weakness — carrying only ~5 days' provisions. He advocated a scorched-earth strategy to starve Alexander out. After Halicarnassus, Memnon commanded the entire Persian navy and began recapturing islands, threatening Alexander's supply lines from the Hellespont. His death in 333 BC (during or after the siege of Mytilene) is one of the campaign's great "what ifs" — had he lived, he might have cut Alexander's supply lines or even invaded Greece while Alexander was in Asia.
Q9: How did Alexander maintain control over conquered territories, and why does the lecturer argue this was a campaign "about ownership, not destruction"?
Analysing
Alexander kept existing Persian administrative structures (the satrapal system) largely intact, replacing satraps with his own men but leaving taxation and governance functioning as before. Cities that surrendered peacefully experienced little change in daily life. After Issus, he adopted Darius's captured family rather than mistreating them, making himself their patriarch. He took titles like "Lord of Asia" rather than "Great King," deliberately positioning himself as the new ruler of the Persian system rather than its destroyer. Plutarch claims he was proclaimed "King of Asia" after Issus.
Q10: Which ancient source on Alexander's campaigns is considered most reliable and why?
Remembering
Arrian's Anabasis is considered most reliable. Though written in the 2nd century AD (about 400 years after Alexander), Arrian based his account on near-contemporary sources (Ptolemy and Aristobulus) and is generally careful with sources. Plutarch's Life is more biographical and anecdotal. All sources carry risks of distortion and legend.
Q11: Compare: Was the Persian Empire conquered because of Alexander's genius or Persia's weakness?
Evaluating
Open-ended answer. Strong responses address both factors: Alexander's military innovations (sarissa, phalanx, cavalry tactics), personal leadership, and strategic vision were extraordinary. AND the Persian Empire may have been overextended, facing internal strains, or underestimating the threat. The best answer avoids false dichotomy — both factors mattered.
Q12: What do Persian sources reveal about the conquest that Greek sources often downplay?
Understanding
Persian sources emphasize the catastrophic human and cultural costs of conquest — cities burned, the imperial system destroyed, Persian dominance in the Near East ended. While Greek sources celebrate Alexander's triumph, Persian sources show conquest as devastating loss. Historians like Briant emphasize this "Persian perspective" that Greek sources marginalize.
