1
Why Study Alexander?
▼

Alexander stands at one of the most important crossroads in history—the boundary between two eras with fundamentally different characteristics.

The Two Eras

His death in 323 BC marks the conventional divide between the Classical period and the Hellenistic period (323–31 BC).

Classical Period

Greek poleis (independent city-states) as dominant political form. Athens and Sparta compete for leadership. Persian Empire stands as the powerful eastern superpower. Warfare between Greeks and Persia (Persian Wars). Culture remains distinctly Greek.

Hellenistic Period (323–31 BC)

Alexander's conquest creates vast, centralized kingdoms. Greek culture spreads to Egypt, Persia, India, and beyond. Fusion of Greek and Persian cultural elements. Kings rule through absolute power, not polis autonomy. Period ends with Rome's conquest of Egypt at the Battle of Actium.

Why this matters: Alexander didn't just conquer—he fundamentally transformed how the Mediterranean and Near Eastern world were organized. He took Classical Greek culture and refashioned it into something new by incorporating Persian Empire elements and institutions. Understanding this transformation is central to studying the Hellenistic world.

Key insight: The Hellenistic period was not merely a continuation of Classical Greece. It represented a wholesale reimagining of political power, cultural identity, and territorial organization.
2
The Alexander Mosaic
▼

One of our most vivid visual representations of Alexander comes from an unexpected place: the floor of a Roman house in Pompeii.

The Discovery

The famous Alexander Mosaic was discovered in the House of the Faun in Pompeii. It depicts either the Battle of Gaugamela (331 BC) or the Battle of Issus (333 BC)—historians debate which. The mosaic shows Alexander on horseback, fearless and dynamic, facing Darius III, the Persian king.

Layers of Interpretation

This single image demonstrates a crucial principle in studying Alexander: material evidence is always filtered through choices. Consider the layers:

  • An original Greek painting (4th century BC) showing a contemporary view
  • Copied or adapted by Roman artisans (1st century AD) for a Pompeian home
  • Preserved in ash when Vesuvius erupted (79 AD)
  • Excavated and displayed (18th century onward)
  • Interpreted by modern historians with modern biases

Standard Portrait Features

In the mosaic and across ancient coinage and sculpture, Alexander is consistently depicted with these features:

  • Curly or wavy hair
  • Youthful face (even in images made near the end of his life)
  • Clean-shaven (Persian style influenced by his time in the East)
  • Idealized features (more perfect than realistic)
  • Often shown with head slightly tilted or turned
Material evidence is powerful but always filtered through the artist's choices and later copying. The mosaic tells us about both Alexander's legend and Roman attitudes toward power.
3
Six Lost Sources
▼

Here's a stark reality: Alexander left no written records that survive. None. Not a single letter, diary, or official document from his life remains intact.

What We Lost

According to ancient citations and fragments, these six categories of primary source material once existed:

  1. First-hand campaign documentation — daily records of military movements and sieges
  2. Letters from Alexander — correspondence to generals, cities, and his mother Olympias
  3. Court records — administrative decisions and decrees
  4. Earlier historical accounts — histories written by contemporary historians
  5. Military records — troop deployments, logistics, casualties
  6. Administrative materials — tax records, city charters, personnel lists

The Fundamental Challenge

Everything we know about Alexander comes from secondhand accounts written centuries after his death. We never read the original sources. Instead, we read about the original sources, filtered through the interpretations and biases of later authors.

Think of it this way: Imagine trying to write a biography using only reviews of a book, never reading the book itself. That's the position we're in with Alexander—we're always one step removed from the original evidence.

This doesn't make studying Alexander impossible—but it fundamentally shapes how we approach the evidence and the conclusions we can draw.

4
Four Extant Authors
▼

Since no original Alexander documents survive, we rely on four ancient authors whose works have been preserved. Each offers a different perspective.

Arrian — Anabasis of Alexander

  • Who: Greek historian (c. 86–160 AD), wrote ~400 years after Alexander
  • Approach: Military-focused narrative of Alexander's campaigns
  • Sources: Explicitly based on two eyewitness accounts: Ptolemy (general, later Egypt's king) and Aristobulus (engineer in Alexander's army)
  • Reliability: Generally considered the most detailed and credible account

Plutarch — Life of Alexander

  • Who: Greek biographer (c. 45–120 AD), ~450 years after Alexander
  • Approach: Biographical focus on character, philosophy, and moral lessons
  • Sources: Various lost accounts, oral traditions, and other biographies
  • Bias: Interested in philosophical and moral themes over military detail

Diodorus Siculus — Bibliotheca Historica

  • Who: Greek historian (c. 90–30 BC), earlier but less detailed
  • Approach: Universal history from creation to his own time
  • Sources: Earlier historians and compilations
  • Limitation: Alexander is one topic among many; less comprehensive

Curtius Rufus — Historiae Alexandri Magni

  • Who: Roman historian (1st century AD), date uncertain
  • Approach: Rhetorical and dramatic style, emphasizes personality
  • Language: Written in Latin (unique among our major sources)
  • Reliability: More dramatic, sometimes less factually precise

Quick Check

Question: Which two eyewitness sources did Arrian explicitly rely on?

Answer: Ptolemy and Aristobulus

Key point: All four authors wrote long after Alexander's lifetime. The closest—Diodorus—was born ~60 years after Alexander died. This temporal distance means their accounts are shaped by the historical events that happened between Alexander's lifetime and theirs.

5
Four Questions for Source Analysis
▼

No matter what source you're examining—whether it's Arrian, a mosaic, a coin, or an inscription—you should always ask the same four critical questions:

Question 1: Who wrote it and when?

Why it matters: Temporal distance from events shapes interpretation. An account written 50 years later includes memories and biases that developed in the interim. Ask about the author's background, education, and time period.

Question 2: What was their purpose?

Why it matters: A history meant to entertain differs from one meant to instruct. An inscription celebrating a king's power has different biases than a philosophical biography. Purpose shapes what details are included and how they're framed.

Question 3: What sources did THEY use?

Why it matters: You can only trust an author as far as you trust their sources. If they relied on eyewitnesses, they're more credible. If they relied on hearsay or propaganda, less so. This is the "chain of evidence" problem.

Question 4: What biases might be present?

Why it matters: All sources have biases—cultural, political, and personal. A Greek author views Alexander differently than a Persian one. A supporter views him differently than a critic. Identifying biases doesn't invalidate a source; it contextualizes it.

Mini-Exercise: Apply to Arrian

Apply the four questions to Arrian's Anabasis:

1. Who and when? Arrian, Greek historian, ~130 AD (~450 years after Alexander died)

2. Purpose? Military narrative—detailed record of Alexander's campaigns and conquests

3. Sources? Ptolemy (eyewitness, became king of Egypt, had bias toward legitimacy) and Aristobulus (engineer, may have emphasized technical achievements)

4. Biases? Greek cultural perspective; likely respected monarchical power; may have emphasized military genius over political reality; inherited any biases from Ptolemy and Aristobulus

These four questions form the foundation of source criticism. Master them, and you'll be able to evaluate any evidence about Alexander.

6
Material Evidence vs Literary Sources
▼

When we study Alexander, we combine two fundamentally different types of evidence. Each has strengths and weaknesses.

Material Evidence

Examples: Coins, mosaics, sculptures, inscriptions, tombs, architectural remains, pottery

Strengths: Less prone to authorial agenda and bias; represents actual decisions and choices made at the time; can contradict literary accounts

Weaknesses: Hard to interpret without context; fragmented and incomplete; affected by preservation accidents; requires inference

Literary Sources

Examples: Arrian, Plutarch, Diodorus, Curtius, fragments from lost works

Strengths: Provides narrative and context; explains motivations and consequences; offers multiple perspectives

Weaknesses: Written long after events; filtered through author's agenda; cannot verify sources; may contain propaganda or distortion

Why You Need Both

The best historical understanding combines both types. Literary sources tell you what happened and why. Material evidence shows you what people actually valued and chose to build. When they agree, you can be more confident. When they conflict, you have a puzzle to solve—which may reveal important truths about bias in the literary record.

Example: Coins as Evidence

Alexander's coins show his portrait (power and prestige), Greek symbols (cultural identity), and spread across the known world (proving reach of his empire). Literary sources might exaggerate his popularity or minimize local resistance. Coins offer silent testimony to how Alexander presented himself and how widely that image was distributed.

Tip: In essays and discussions, always distinguish between types of evidence. "The sources say..." means literary sources. "Material evidence shows..." is coins, inscriptions, etc. Being precise strengthens your arguments.

Ancient Text Referencing
▼

When you read historical scholarship about Alexander, you'll see citations that look unfamiliar. Understanding this system is essential for academic work in ancient history.

The Standard Citation Format

Ancient texts use a reference system independent of page numbers (texts predate printing). The standard format is:

Author Abbreviation, Work Abbreviation, Book.Chapter.Section

Common Examples

  • Arr. Anab. 1.12.1 — Arrian, Anabasis, Book 1, Chapter 12, Section 1
  • Plut. Alex. 10.2 — Plutarch, Life of Alexander, Chapter 10, Section 2
  • Diod. 17.3.4 — Diodorus Siculus, Book 17, Chapter 3, Section 4
  • Curt. 4.6.3 — Curtius Rufus, Book 4, Chapter 6, Section 3

Why This Matters

This system allows scholars to reference the same passage regardless of which translation or edition they use. Whether reading an ancient Greek manuscript, a 17th-century Latin edition, or a modern English translation, "Arr. Anab. 1.12.1" refers to the identical passage. This makes cross-referencing possible and allows precise academic discussion.

Tip for essays: Always use the standard ancient referencing convention when citing sources from Arrian, Plutarch, and other ancient authors. This demonstrates mastery of the material and makes your work verifiable. Example: "According to Arrian, Alexander was crowned king in Babylon (Arr. Anab. 7.22.1)."

Different translations may use slightly different numbering systems, so checking the edition being used is important. But the standard is widely consistent across modern scholarship.

The Classical World Before Alexander
▼

To understand Alexander's impact, you must first understand what world he inherited.

The Greek Polis System

Classical Greece was not a unified state. Instead, it consisted of independent city-states called poleis (plural of polis), each with its own government, laws, army, and foreign policy. Major poleis included Athens, Sparta, Thebes, Corinth, and Syracuse (in Sicily).

Athens and Sparta: The Superpowers

By the 4th century BC, two poleis dominated Greek political thinking:

  • Athens: Democratic, maritime power, intellectual and cultural center, naval empire
  • Sparta: Oligarchic, land-based military power, strict discipline, feared warrior culture

These two had fought the Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC) for supremacy, leaving Greece weakened and fractured.

The Persian Empire: Eastern Superpower

While Greek poleis competed among themselves, the Achaemenid Persian Empire ruled a vast territory stretching from Egypt to India. The Persians had invaded Greece twice (490 and 480 BC), and Greek-Persian conflict shaped political thinking across the Mediterranean.

Constant Warfare

Classical Greece was characterized by constant inter-polis conflict. Wars between rival cities were routine. Simultaneously, Greek cities feared and fought against Persian expansion. This created a fragmented, unstable political landscape.

What Alexander Changed

Alexander inherited this fractured world in 336 BC. His Macedonian kingdom was considered semi-barbarian by the southern Greek poleis. Within 13 years, he conquered the Persian Empire, unified Greek and Persian territories, and created a new political order. The classical world of independent city-states would never recover.

Key insight: Alexander didn't create stability where chaos reigned—Greek poleis would have remained divided and competitive without him. But he imposed a centralized monarchical order that fundamentally displaced the polis as the center of political life.

Key Geography
▼

To understand Alexander's campaigns and the Hellenistic world, you must know the geography. The following map shows the key locations you should be familiar with.

Locations You Need to Know

  • Amphipolis: Alexander's birthplace, Macedonian city
  • Macedon: Alexander's home kingdom in northern Greece
  • Chaeronea: Site of Alexander's first major victory (338 BC)
  • Athens & Thebes: Major Greek city-states Alexander subdued
  • Sparta: The other Greek superpower, notably not conquered directly
  • Troy: Alexander visited the ruins of ancient Troy in Asia Minor
  • Siwah Oasis: In Egypt, where the oracle declared Alexander a pharaoh/son of Amun
  • Babylon: Capital of the Persian Empire and Alexander's capital
  • Persian Empire: Stretched from Egypt to India—Alexander's target
Important Reminder: The EFT Map Quiz (3% of your grade) is closed-book in tutorials, Week 3. You will need to identify these locations and others on a map. Start learning the geography now—don't leave this until the last minute. Regular practice will make this automatic.

Why Geography Matters

Alexander's campaigns make sense only if you can visualize the distances and routes. Understanding geography helps you grasp why certain battles happened where they did, why logistics were challenging, and how Alexander managed to maintain control across such vast distances.

Putting It All Together
▼

You've now learned the foundations needed for this course. Let's consolidate what you've learned and look ahead.

Skills You've Developed This Week

By studying the sources, evidence, and context, you've practiced:

  • Source criticism: Asking the four analytical questions about any ancient text or artifact
  • Evidence evaluation: Understanding the strengths and limitations of material vs. literary evidence
  • Temporal awareness: Recognizing how distance in time shapes what we know
  • Context understanding: Seeing Alexander not in isolation but within the Classical world he inherited
  • Geographic literacy: Mapping key locations essential for understanding Alexander's campaigns

How to Read Primary Sources Critically

When you encounter a passage from Arrian, Plutarch, or other ancient authors in lectures or readings:

  1. Note the source using correct ancient referencing (Arr. Anab. X.Y.Z)
  2. Ask: Who wrote this, and when? (temporal distance matters)
  3. Ask: What was their purpose? (this shapes content selection)
  4. Ask: What's their source material? (chain of evidence)
  5. Identify possible biases: cultural, political, personal
  6. Compare with material evidence if available (coins, inscriptions, etc.)
  7. Synthesize: What can we cautiously conclude?

Combining Evidence Types

The best historical arguments use both material and literary evidence:

  • Literary sources give you narrative and motivation
  • Material evidence shows what people actually valued and built
  • When they agree, confidence is high
  • When they disagree, you've found a puzzle worth investigating
Core insight for this course: Alexander is not a single historical figure but a collection of sources and evidence, each filtered through time, bias, and the choices of those who preserved or created them. Understanding Alexander means understanding these filters, not removing them.

Why is the Hellenistic period described as a "refashioning" of Classical Greek culture?
UNDERSTANDING
