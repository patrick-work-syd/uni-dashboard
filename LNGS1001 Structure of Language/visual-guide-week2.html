From Spelling to Sound

Why we need phonetics
›

Week 1 showed that writing ≠ sound. This week we learn how to scientifically describe speech sounds. Phonetics is the empirical study of speech sounds — production (articulatory), transmission (acoustic), and perception (auditory). We focus on articulatory phonetics: how we physically make sounds using our lungs, larynx, and vocal tract.

Everyday notion

I make sounds with my mouth

Linguistic definition

Speech is pulmonic egressive airflow modified by articulators in the supralaryngeal vocal tract

This week, we focus specifically on consonants — sounds made by obstructing, narrowing, or diverting airflow. Next week we'll tackle vowels, which have unobstructed airflow.

✎ Quick check
What are the three branches of phonetics, and which one does this course focus on?
The three branches are: (1) Articulatory phonetics — how we produce sounds; (2) Acoustic phonetics — how sound travels and what its physical properties are; (3) Auditory/perceptual phonetics — how we hear and perceive sounds. This course focuses on articulatory phonetics — understanding how the vocal tract makes consonants and vowels.
2

The Vocal Tract

Your personal sound lab
›

Air from your lungs passes through the larynx (containing vocal folds), then through the supralaryngeal vocal tract. We modify this airflow using active articulators (tongue, lips — things that move) and passive articulators (teeth, hard palate, alveolar ridge — things that stay still).

💡
Pro tip: Run your finger along the roof of your mouth from your teeth backward. You'll feel the hard palate, then a soft bump (velar area). These are the places where your tongue contacts to make different sounds.
✎ Quick check
Name two active articulators and two passive articulators.
Active articulators (things that move): tongue, lips, vocal folds. Passive articulators (things that stay still): teeth, hard palate, alveolar ridge, pharyngeal walls. The key distinction is that active articulators move toward a passive one to create an obstruction or narrowing.
3

Voicing

Vocal fold vibration makes all the difference
›

Place your hand on your throat. Say "ssss" — no vibration (voiceless). Say "zzzz" — vibration (voiced). The only difference between /s/ and /z/ is whether your vocal folds vibrate. Same distinction applies to /f/ vs /v/, /p/ vs /b/, /t/ vs /d/.

Everyday observation

Some sounds buzz in your throat

Linguistic definition

Voiced sounds involve vibration of the vocal folds; voiceless sounds do not

✋
The voicing test: On the IPA chart, voiced sounds appear on the RIGHT side of a cell, voiceless on the LEFT. This matters because many consonants differ ONLY in voicing — they are identical in place and manner.
💡
Try this: Say "a pair" slowly. You'll feel vibration stop at the /p/ and restart at the vowel. Now say "a bear" — vibration continues the whole way through. Your vocal folds shut down for voiceless /p/ but stay vibrating for voiced /b/.
✎ Quick check
Put your hand on your throat and say /f/ then /v/. What's the single physical difference between them?
/f/ is voiceless (no vibration in your throat); /v/ is voiced (you feel vibration). Both are made by placing your lower lip against your upper teeth and creating turbulent airflow. The ONLY difference is voicing — whether the vocal folds vibrate. They are a voiced–voiceless pair.
4

Aspiration

The puff of air you never noticed
›

Hold your hand in front of your mouth. Say "pair" — feel a puff of air on the /p/. Now say "spare" — no puff on the /p/. The first is aspirated [pʰ], the second is unaspirated [p]. In English, voiceless stops (/p/, /t/, /k/) are aspirated at the start of a word but unaspirated after /s/. Aspiration is marked with a superscript ʰ.

Naive view

The P in "pin" and "spin" sound the same

Phonetic reality

"Pin" /pʰɪn/ has aspirated [pʰ]; "spin" /spɪn/ has unaspirated [p]. They're acoustically different, but English treats them as the same phoneme

⚠️
Aspiration is predictable in English: You don't need to learn which words are aspirated. The rule is automatic: voiceless stops are aspirated word-initially (and before stressed vowels) but unaspirated after /s/. This is why aspiration is not contrastive in English — it's determined by the environment.
✎ Quick check
Say "top" and "stop". Which has an aspirated /t/? How could you test this?
"Top" has aspirated [tʰ]; "stop" has unaspirated [t]. Test it: hold your hand in front of your mouth. You'll feel a puff on the /t/ in "top" (because it's word-initial) but not in "stop" (because it follows /s/). Aspiration = a puff of voiceless air between the stop release and the vowel onset.
5

Place of Articulation

WHERE you obstruct the air
›

Consonants are classified by WHERE in the mouth you obstruct the airflow. English uses most major places. Run your tongue back through your mouth and notice where it makes contact:

Bilabial: Both lips. Labiodental: Lower lip touches upper teeth. Dental/Alveolar: Tongue tip touches teeth or the ridge just behind them (most common places in English). Post-alveolar: Slightly behind the alveolar ridge (retroflex in some languages; in English, /ʃ/ and /ʒ/). Palatal: Tongue body against hard palate (/j/ as in "yes"). Velar: Tongue body against soft palate/velum (/k, g, ŋ/). Glottal: Vocal folds (/h/ = breathy, /ʔ/ = complete closure).

✎ Quick check
Feel where your tongue is when you say /t/. Now say /k/. Describe the difference in place of articulation.
/t/ is alveolar — your tongue tip touches the ridge just behind your upper teeth. /k/ is velar — your tongue body moves back and contacts the soft palate (velum). The place of articulation moves from the front of the mouth (alveolar) to the back (velar).
6

Manner of Articulation

HOW you obstruct the air
›

While place tells us WHERE, manner tells us HOW we obstruct the airflow. The main manners in English:

  • Stops/Plosives: Complete blockage then release (/p, b, t, d, k, g/)
  • Fricatives: Narrow gap creates turbulence (/f, v, θ, ð, s, z, ʃ, ʒ, h/)
  • Affricates: Stop + fricative combined (/tʃ/ as in "church", /dʒ/ as in "judge")
  • Nasals: Air diverted through nose (/m, n, ŋ/)
  • Approximants/Liquids: Slight narrowing, no turbulence (/l, r, w, j/)
💡
Remember the difference: Say /s/ (fricative — continuous hissing sound) vs. /t/ (stop — quick blockage and release). Manner describes how the airflow is modified: completely stopped, narrowed with turbulence, or diverted.
✎ Quick check
What manner of articulation do /m/, /n/, and /ŋ/ share? What happens if you plug your nose while saying them?
They are all nasals — oral airflow is blocked, but air escapes through the nasal cavity. If you plug your nose, you can't say them properly (they'll sound like /d, d, g/ instead, depending on the place). The nasal manner is defined by velum lowering, allowing air to flow nasally.
7

Reading the IPA Consonant Chart

Place × Manner × Voicing
›

The IPA consonant chart is a grid. Columns = place of articulation (left to right: bilabial → glottal). Rows = manner of articulation (top to bottom: stop → approximant). Within each cell: left = voiceless, right = voiced.

To describe any consonant, give three features: voicing + place + manner. Example: /b/ = voiced bilabial stop. /ʃ/ = voiceless post-alveolar fricative. /tʃ/ = voiceless post-alveolar affricate.

💡
You don't need to memorise the entire chart. Understand the system — then you can always look up any sound by finding its place (column) and manner (row). The beauty of the IPA is that it's systematic.
✎ Quick check
Using the IPA chart, describe /d/ with three terms (voicing, place, manner). Now do the same for /f/.
/d/ = voiced alveolar stop. /f/ = voiceless labiodental fricative. To find them: /d/ is in the stop row, alveolar column, right side (voiced). /f/ is in the fricative row, labiodental column, left side (voiceless).
8

English Consonants in IPA

The sounds you already make
›

English has approximately 24 consonant phonemes. Some IPA symbols match English letters (/p, b, t, d, k, g, m, n, f, v, s, z, h, l, r, w, j/). Others look unfamiliar:

  • /θ/ = "th" in "thin" (voiceless dental fricative)
  • /ð/ = "th" in "this" (voiced dental fricative)
  • /ʃ/ = "sh" in "ship" (voiceless post-alveolar fricative)
  • /ʒ/ = "s" in "measure" (voiced post-alveolar fricative)
  • /tʃ/ = "ch" in "church" (voiceless post-alveolar affricate)
  • /dʒ/ = "j" in "judge" (voiced post-alveolar affricate)
  • /ŋ/ = "ng" in "sing" (voiced velar nasal)
  • /ʔ/ = glottal stop in "uh-oh" (voiceless glottal stop)
⚠️
Don't be fooled by familiar letters! In IPA, /j/ = the 'y' sound in "yes" (NOT the English 'j' sound, which is /dʒ/). Similarly, /ŋ/ is a single consonant (the "ng" sound), not two separate sounds.
✎ Quick check
Transcribe the word "church" in IPA. How many distinct sounds does it have?
/tʃɜːrtʃ/ (or /tʃɝtʃ/ in American English). It has 5 sounds: /tʃ/ (affricate) + /ɜː/ (vowel) + /r/ + /t/ + /ʃ/. The spelling suggests 6 letters (c-h-u-r-c-h), but phonetically there are only 5 sounds. The "ch" at the beginning and end are each single affricates, not separate sounds.
9

Sounds English Doesn't Have

Beyond your native inventory
›

The IPA covers ALL human language sounds, not just English. Languages like French use uvular /ʁ/ (the French 'r'). Arabic has pharyngeal sounds. Many African and Australian languages use retroflex consonants. Click consonants (found in languages like Zulu and !Xóõ) use a different airstream mechanism entirely — they're not pulmonic (lung-powered) sounds.

The grey/shaded areas on the full IPA chart represent sounds that are physically impossible. For example, you can't make a nasal at the glottal place because the velum (soft palate) would need to be both raised (to block the mouth) and lowered (to let air out the nose) simultaneously.

👍
Why this matters: The IPA doesn't represent just English sounds — it represents the full space of possible human sounds. When you encounter a new language, the IPA helps you know what its sounds might be, even if you've never heard them before.
✎ Quick check
Why are some cells shaded on the IPA chart? Give an example of a sound combination that is physically impossible.
Shaded areas represent physically impossible sound combinations. Example: a nasal glottal sound is impossible because to make a nasal, the velum must lower (to let air out the nose), but the glottal place requires the vocal folds to close and vibrate. You can't have both conditions simultaneously. The vocal tract anatomy constrains which consonants can exist.
10

Putting It All Together

Three-part descriptions for any consonant
›

For any consonant in any language, you can give a complete description: (1) Voicing — voiced or voiceless, (2) Place — where the obstruction occurs, (3) Manner — how the airflow is modified.

Example chain: "The /z/ in 'zoo'" = voiced alveolar fricative. "The /tʃ/ in 'chip'" = voiceless post-alveolar affricate. "The /ŋ/ in 'sing'" = voiced velar nasal. This three-part system is how linguists worldwide describe and compare sounds across all 7,000+ languages. No matter what sound you encounter, you can break it down into these three features.

💡
Pro tip: Always describe consonants in the order (Voicing, Place, Manner). This makes your descriptions consistent and easy to compare. If you can describe a sound, you can transcribe it. If you can transcribe it, you can teach someone to pronounce it.

Week 2 in one sentence

Consonants are sounds made by obstructing or narrowing airflow; every consonant in every language can be described by three articulatory features — voicing (whether vocal folds vibrate), place (where in the mouth the obstruction occurs), and manner (how the air is obstructed) — which are systematically displayed on the IPA consonant chart.

✎ Quick check
Describe these three sounds in full IPA terminology: the first sound in "nice", the last sound in "sing", and the middle consonant in "measure".
(1) First sound in "nice" = /n/ = voiced alveolar nasal. (2) Last sound in "sing" = /ŋ/ = voiced velar nasal. (3) Middle consonant in "measure" = /ʒ/ = voiced post-alveolar fricative.
📝 Week 2 Self-Test
10 exam-style questions. Try to answer each one before revealing. These cover the full study guide.
Q1Understanding

What are the three parameters used to classify consonants in the IPA?

Voicing (voiced or voiceless), place of articulation (where the obstruction occurs), and manner of articulation (how the airflow is modified).
Q2Understanding

Name the six major places of articulation used in English consonants.

Bilabial, labiodental, dental/alveolar, post-alveolar, palatal, velar (and glottal for /h/ and /ʔ/).
Q3Understanding

Explain the difference between a stop and a fricative, with examples.

A stop involves complete blockage of airflow followed by release (e.g., /p/, /t/, /k/). A fricative involves a narrow gap that creates turbulent airflow (e.g., /f/, /s/, /ʃ/). An affricate combines both: a stop release into a fricative (e.g., /tʃ/).
Q4Understanding

Why is aspiration in English predictable rather than contrastive?

In English, aspiration follows a rule: voiceless stops are aspirated word-initially (e.g., [pʰɪn] in "pin") but unaspirated after /s/ (e.g., [spɪn] in "spin"). Since aspiration is predictable from the environment, it doesn't change meaning — 'pin' and 'spin' both contain /p/, just with different aspiration.
Q5Understanding

What is the difference between an active and a passive articulator?

An active articulator moves to create the obstruction (e.g., tongue, lips). A passive articulator stays still while the active one approaches it (e.g., teeth, alveolar ridge, hard palate).
Q6Apply

Transcribe the word "think" in IPA and describe each consonant.

/θɪŋk/. /θ/ = voiceless dental fricative. /ŋ/ = voiced velar nasal. /k/ = voiceless velar stop.
Q7Apply

Your friend claims /p/ and /b/ are completely different sounds. A linguist would say they're closely related. Explain why.

They are identical in place (bilabial) and manner (stop). The ONLY difference is voicing: /p/ is voiceless and /b/ is voiced. The IPA chart reflects this by putting them in the same cell, with /p/ on the left (voiceless) and /b/ on the right (voiced).
Q8Apply

A speaker of Thai produces a sound that is a voiced aspirated bilabial stop. Where would this appear on the IPA chart?

It would be in the bilabial column, stop row, on the voiced (right) side. Aspiration would be marked with a superscript ʰ diacritic. This sound doesn't exist in English but is phonemic in Thai.
Q9Analyse

English has both /θ/ (as in "thin") and /ð/ (as in "this"). Many languages lack these sounds entirely. What does this tell us about the relationship between the IPA and any particular language?

The IPA represents the full possibility space of human speech sounds, not the inventory of any single language. Each language selects a subset of possible sounds. English happens to use dental fricatives, but most languages don't. The IPA must be comprehensive precisely because different languages make different selections.
Q10Analyse

If you were trying to describe a consonant from a language you'd never heard before, and you could only observe the speaker's mouth, which of the three parameters (voicing, place, manner) would be hardest to determine? Why?

Voicing would be hardest because it involves vocal fold vibration inside the larynx, which is not directly visible. Place and manner are often visible — you can see lip movements (bilabial), tongue position (dental/alveolar), and whether airflow is fully stopped or continuous. Voicing requires either touching the throat or using acoustic instruments.
