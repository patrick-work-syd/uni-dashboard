Vowels: No Obstruction

Open airflow, shaped by tongue and lips
›

Consonants obstruct airflow; vowels don't. When you say "ahh," air flows freely — you're shaping the sound by moving your tongue position and rounding your lips, not blocking anything. Vowels are almost always voiced (vocal folds vibrating). They form the nucleus of every syllable.

Everyday idea

Vowels are A, E, I, O, U

Linguistic definition

Vowels are speech sounds with unimpeded airflow, classified by tongue height, tongue frontness/backness, and lip rounding

The key insight: vowels are characterised by articulation freedom, not by arbitrary letter categories. Every vowel can be uniquely described by three parameters: how high your tongue is, where it sits front-to-back, and whether your lips round.

✎ Quick check
Say /ɑː/ (as in 'father') and then /s/. What physical difference do you feel in how the air moves?
In /ɑː/, air flows freely through the mouth with your tongue low. In /s/, you constrict the airflow with your tongue near the alveolar ridge, creating friction. /ɑː/ is unobstructed; /s/ is obstructed.
2

The Vowel Trapezoid

A map of your mouth
›

The vowel chart is a schematic of tongue position inside your mouth:

Top = tongue high (close vowels like /i/ in "beat"). Bottom = tongue low (open vowels like /ɑ/ in "father"). Left = tongue front (/i/). Right = tongue back (/u/ in "boot"). Within each position, left symbol = unrounded lips, right symbol = rounded lips. The schwa /ə/ sits dead centre — your tongue is nowhere in particular.

💡
Mirror trick: Look at yourself in your phone camera. Say 'beat' then 'boot' — watch your lips go from spread to round. Say 'beat' then 'bat' — watch your jaw drop as your tongue lowers.
✎ Quick check
Where on the vowel trapezoid would you place the vowel in 'boot'? Describe its height, backness, and lip rounding.
/uː/ is high (close), back, and rounded. It sits in the top-right area of the chart — high tongue position, pulled toward the back, with rounded lips.
3

Monophthongs vs Diphthongs

Single targets vs gliding vowels
›

A monophthong has one tongue position (e.g., /iː/ in "beat" — tongue stays high and front). A diphthong involves movement between two positions (e.g., /aɪ/ in "bite" — tongue starts low then glides up).

Australian English is rich in diphthongs: /eɪ/ (bay), /aɪ/ (bite), /ɔɪ/ (boy), /əʉ/ (boat), /æɔ/ (bout). English has 18 distinct vowel sounds — far more than the 5 letters A, E, I, O, U suggest.

✎ Quick check
Say 'boy' very slowly. Can you identify the two vowel targets your tongue moves between?
/ɔɪ/: starts with an open back rounded vowel /ɔ/, then glides to a high front vowel /ɪ/. Your tongue moves from the back-low position to the front-high position.

Australian English Vowels

18 vowels from 5 letters
›

The Harrington-Cox-Evans (HCE) transcription system is the standard for Australian English. Use this when transcribing the Assignment 1 recordings.

Vowels are variable — even within Sydney, speakers differ. The HCE system captures the most common General Australian English patterns. When you transcribe the Assignment 1 recordings, focus on what you actually hear in the speaker's voice, not what standard dictionaries might suggest.

⚠️
Do NOT use ChatGPT to transcribe your assignment. The recordings capture a specific speaker's pronunciation. Only listening will give you the right transcription.
✎ Quick check
How many monophthong vowels does General Australian English have? Why is this surprising given English has only 5 vowel letters?
11 monophthongs. This is surprising because English has only 5 vowel letters (a, e, i, o, u), but 11 distinct vowel sounds. The letters are arbitrary — they don't map cleanly to vowels. Words spelled with the same vowel letter can sound completely different: "beat" /iː/ vs "bed" /e/.
5

Cross-Linguistic Vowel Inventories

Different languages, different selections
›

Languages choose different subsets of the vowel space. No language uses ALL possible vowels — each selects a subset based on its phonological system.

Getting the wrong vowel in these languages changes meaning entirely. This is why French speakers pronounce English words differently — they project their own vowel system onto English sounds.

✎ Quick check
French has the vowel /y/ (high front rounded). English has /iː/ (high front unrounded) and /uː/ (high back rounded). Describe what makes /y/ different from both.
/y/ is HIGH (like /iː/) and ROUNDED (like /uː/), but combines both properties. English separates these: high vowels are either front-unrounded (/iː/) or back-rounded (/uː/). /y/ is a front-rounded vowel — the combination doesn't exist in English.
6

Syllable Structure

Onset, nucleus, coda
›

A syllable has up to three parts: onset (initial consonant(s)), nucleus (usually a vowel — the core), and coda (final consonant(s)). Only the nucleus is required.

In English, only the nucleus is required — "oh" /əʊ/ is a valid syllable with no onset or coda. But English allows complex structures: "strengths" /stɹɛŋkθs/ has 3 onset consonants and 4 coda consonants in a single syllable.

✎ Quick check
Break the word 'plant' into onset, nucleus, and coda. What is the rhyme?
Onset: /pl/, nucleus: /æ/, coda: /nt/. Rhyme: /ænt/ (nucleus + coda together).
7

Sonority & the Sonorant Sandwich

Why syllables are shaped the way they are
›

Syllables follow a sonority pattern: they rise from the edges to a peak at the nucleus. Vowels are the most sonorant (loudest, most resonant). Stops are the least sonorant. This is why syllable nuclei are almost always vowels, and why consonant clusters at the edges follow specific patterns.

✎ Quick check
Why can't English have a word that starts with /mk/? Think about the sonority hierarchy.
/m/ (nasal) has higher sonority than /k/ (stop). English requires consonant clusters to rise in sonority toward the nucleus. Starting with /mk/ would require sonority to drop (from /m/ to /k/), which violates the sonority principle.
8

Stress

Making syllables stand out
›

Stress = making one syllable more prominent than others, through loudness, pitch, duration, or clearer articulation. In English, stress is phonemic — it changes meaning:

Informal ↔ Formal: "I said it louder" ↔ "The stressed syllable has greater amplitude, higher F0, longer duration, and less vowel reduction."

Unstressed syllables reduce toward schwa: phoTOGraphy (the vowels shift). Stress placement changes with suffixes.

✎ Quick check
The word 'record' can be a noun or a verb. How does stress distinguish them? What happens to the vowels?
Noun: /'ɹɛ.kɔɹd/ (stress on first syllable). Verb: /ɹə.'kɔɹd/ (stress on second syllable). In the noun, the first vowel is clear /ɛ/. In the verb, the first vowel reduces toward schwa /ə/.
9

Rhythm: Stress-Timed vs Syllable-Timed

Why English and French sound so different
›

English is stress-timed: stressed syllables occur at roughly equal intervals, and unstressed syllables get squished between them. French is syllable-timed: each syllable takes roughly the same duration.

This is why early text-to-speech (like old Siri) sounded robotic — it used syllable timing instead of stress timing. Modern systems correct for this.

✎ Quick check
Why would a native French speaker sound 'choppy' to an English listener, and an English speaker sound 'mumbled' to a French listener?
French speakers apply syllable timing to English, making each syllable equally prominent and distinct — sounding choppy/staccato. English speakers compress unstressed syllables in French, making them harder to hear — sounding mumbled/slurred. Each listener expects their own rhythm pattern.
10

Tone Systems

When pitch changes meaning
›

In tone languages, pitch level or pitch movement changes the meaning of a word. Mandarin has 4 tones:

Register tone systems (e.g., Ibibio, Nigeria) use level pitches (high, mid, low). Contour tone systems (e.g., Mandarin) use pitch movement (rising, falling). Pitch accent languages (e.g., Tokyo Japanese) have fixed pitch patterns per word.

Important: English doesn't use tone to distinguish words. We use intonation (pitch at the sentence level), which is different from lexical tone.

✎ Quick check
In Mandarin, mā means 'mother' and mǎ means 'horse'. What type of tone system does Mandarin have — register or contour? Why?
Mandarin has a contour tone system. Tone 1 (mā) is a level high tone (register), but Tone 3 (mǎ) is a falling-rising contour. Contour tones involve pitch movement within the syllable, not just static pitch levels.
11

Voice Quality

Modal, creaky, and breathy
›

Voice quality refers to HOW your vocal folds vibrate:

In English, these are just stylistic/social — they don't change word meaning. But in Gujarati, breathy voice is phonemic (bɑr vs b̤ɑr = different words). In Jalapa Mazatec, there's a three-way contrast: modal, creaky, and breathy voice on the same vowel, each giving a different word.

💡
Why is voice quality "suprasegmental"? Because it overlays the entire utterance (or large chunks of it), not just individual segments. You can apply creaky voice to a whole sentence, affecting all vowels simultaneously.
✎ Quick check
Why is voice quality considered a suprasegmental feature rather than a segmental one?
Voice quality overlays the entire utterance or large chunks of it, not just individual phonemes. You can speak an entire sentence in breathy voice, affecting all the vowels at once. It's a property of how you're phonating (vocal fold motion), not a discrete segment.
12

Intonation

Pitch at the sentence level
›

Intonation uses pitch patterns across whole sentences — not to change word meaning (that's tone), but for pragmatic functions.

Rising intonation = question ("You're coming?"). Falling = statement ("You're coming."). Focus placement changes meaning: "HIS first wife was Swedish" (not someone else's) vs "His FIRST wife was Swedish" (he's had others). Even pause placement matters: "What's that on the road ahead?" vs "What's that on the road? A head?"

Australian English has High Rising Terminal (HRT) — declarative sentences with question-like rising intonation. This is a distinctive feature of Australian English.

✎ Quick check
Say 'The assignments will be easy' as a statement, a question, and with uncertainty. What changes in your pitch each time?
Statement: pitch falls at the end. Question: pitch rises at the end. Uncertainty: pitch may rise slightly or remain relatively flat throughout, without the confident fall. The pitch movement signals attitude/intent.

Week 3 in one sentence

Vowels are unobstructed sounds shaped by tongue position and lip rounding; they form the nucleus of syllables and vary across languages. Suprasegmentals — stress, rhythm, tone, voice quality, and intonation — layer on top of segments to add meaning, emotion, and pragmatic function. Together, they show how language packages meaning at multiple levels simultaneously.

📝 Week 3 Self-Test
10 exam-style questions covering vowels and suprasegmentals. Try to answer each one before revealing.
Q1Understanding

What three parameters classify vowels in the IPA?

Tongue height (close/open), tongue frontness/backness (front/central/back), and lip rounding (rounded/unrounded).
Q2Understanding

What is schwa, and where does it sit on the vowel trapezoid?

Schwa /ə/ is a mid-central vowel — tongue in neutral position, no particular height or frontness, unrounded. It's the most common vowel in unstressed English syllables ('about', 'sofa'). It sits dead centre on the vowel trapezoid.
Q3Understanding

Explain the difference between a monophthong and a diphthong.

A monophthong is a vowel with a single, stable tongue position (e.g., /iː/ in 'beat'). A diphthong involves tongue movement from one position to another within a single syllable (e.g., /aɪ/ in 'bite' — tongue starts low-central and glides to high-front).
Q4Understanding

What are the three components of syllable structure?

Onset (initial consonant(s)), nucleus (the vowel core), and coda (final consonant(s)). The nucleus is the only required element. Nucleus + coda together form the rhyme.
Q5Understanding

What is the 'sonorant sandwich' principle?

Syllables are structured so that sonority rises from the edges to a peak at the nucleus. The most sonorant sound (usually a vowel) is in the middle, with less sonorant sounds (consonants) at the edges. This is why /stɹɛŋkθs/ ('strengths') is a valid English syllable but */ŋkst/ is not.
Q6Apply

The words 'record' (noun) and 'record' (verb) have the same segments. What distinguishes them?

Stress placement: REcord (noun, stress on first syllable) vs reCORD (verb, stress on second). This demonstrates that stress is phonemic in English — it distinguishes grammatical categories and vowel quality.
Q7Apply

In Mandarin, mā (mother), má (hemp), mǎ (horse), mà (scold) differ only in tone. What does this tell us about tone systems?

In tone languages, pitch changes are phonemic — they change word meaning, just like consonants and vowels do in English. Mandarin is a contour tone language: Tone 1 is high level, Tone 2 is rising, Tone 3 is falling-rising, and Tone 4 is falling.
Q8Apply

English uses intonation (pitch at sentence level) but not lexical tone. Japanese uses pitch accent. Mandarin uses contour tones. How would you classify these three languages in terms of how they use pitch?

English: pitch serves pragmatic functions (questions, focus, emotion) but doesn't distinguish word meaning — non-contrastive intonation. Japanese: pitch patterns are fixed per word (pitch accent) — limited tonal contrast. Mandarin: each syllable carries a contrastive tone that changes word meaning — full contour tone system. This represents a continuum from no lexical pitch contrast (English) to full tonal contrast (Mandarin).
Q9Analyse

Voice quality (modal, creaky, breathy) is sociolinguistic in English but phonemic in Gujarati. What does this tell us about the relationship between phonetics and phonology?

The same phonetic feature can serve different functions in different languages. Creaky voice is physically available to all humans, but whether a language uses it to distinguish word meanings (phonemic) or only for social/stylistic purposes (non-phonemic) is language-specific. This illustrates that phonetics provides the universal 'possibility space' while each language's phonology determines which contrasts are meaningful.
Q10Analyse

Australian English has approximately 18 vowel sounds, but English writing uses only 5 vowel letters. Why is spelling so inconsistent compared to transcription?

Spelling is a technology created centuries ago and hasn't been updated to match actual pronunciation. The vowel system has changed (Great Vowel Shift in Early Modern English), but spelling stayed the same. The IPA transcription system was designed specifically to capture phonetic precision, using a unique symbol for each sound. Spelling is arbitrary and historical; the IPA is systematic and phonetically explicit.
