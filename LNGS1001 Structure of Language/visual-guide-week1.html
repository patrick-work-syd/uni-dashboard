What IS Linguistics?

Not what you think
›

Linguistics is the scientific study of human language. It's NOT about learning foreign languages or correcting grammar. A linguist isn't a translator — a linguist is someone who knows about languages, not just someone who knows many languages.

Everyday idea

Someone who speaks lots of languages

Linguistic definition

A scientist who studies the structure and function of human language

Think of it this way: speaking 5 languages fluently is amazing — but it doesn't make you a linguist any more than being a great cook makes you a food chemist.

💡
The question linguists ask: "How does language work?" Not "am I speaking it correctly?" or "which language is best?" Those are prescriptive questions. Linguists are descriptive — we study what IS.
✎ Quick check
In your own words, explain why a polyglot (someone who speaks 5 languages) is not necessarily a linguist.
Speaking many languages is a practical skill. Linguistics is studying how languages work — their structure, patterns, origins. You can speak 5 languages without knowing why they're structured the way they are, and you can be a linguist studying languages you don't actually speak.
2

7,000 Languages and Counting

The scale of human language diversity
›

Roughly 7,000 languages exist today. Only 4% have official status. Papua New Guinea alone has 860+ languages — that's 13% of the world's languages with 0.1% of the world's population. Languages near the equator tend to be more diverse than languages in colder regions.

👍
The baseline for human language groups historically was 10–2,000 people. Way smaller than modern nation-states. This is why "one country, one language" is a very recent invention — and even then, it's rarely true.
✎ Quick check
Why is it misleading to associate one country with one language? Give one example.
Countries are political units; languages are cultural/biological units. Papua New Guinea has one government but 860+ languages. India has 22 official languages. Switzerland has 4 official languages but multiple regional languages. One nation = many languages is the norm, not the exception.
3

No Primitive Languages

Every human language is complex
›

Colonial-era claims that indigenous languages were "primitive" or "meagre" are empirically false. Linguists have studied languages worldwide and found they are all highly complex. Australian Aboriginal languages, for example, have been described as extremely complex by scholars who actually study them.

Historical myth

Some languages are simpler than others

Scientific fact

All human languages exhibit comparable complexity across different structural levels

⚠️
The "primitive language" idea was used to justify mistreatment of indigenous peoples. This is not a political opinion — it's a scientific fact that all human languages are equally complex. When complexity is measured across multiple dimensions (phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics), no language comes out "simpler."
✎ Quick check
What evidence would you use to counter someone claiming that an unwritten language must be "primitive"?
Writing is a technology, not a measure of language complexity. (1) Chinese is one of the oldest written languages but has simple word order; Japanese has elaborate grammar. (2) Unwritten languages can have highly complex sounds, word structure, and grammar. (3) Writing was invented ~5,000 years ago, but all languages have existed for much longer. Complexity and writing are independent.
4

What Makes Human Language Special?

Design features that set us apart
›

Animals communicate, but human language has unique properties called Hockett's design features. Four key ones:

Arbitrariness: The word "dog" has no natural connection to actual dogs — it's arbitrary. Different languages prove this: English uses "dog," French uses "chien," Japanese uses "inu." No universal "dog sound."

Displacement: We can talk about things not present — yesterday, Mars, fictional characters, abstract ideas. Bees dance about food locations (here & now); humans discuss the past and future.

✎ Quick check
Give an example of "displacement" — say something that refers to a time, place, or thing not present in this room.
Any sentence about the past, future, or absent things: "I went to the library yesterday." "Sydney Opera House is beautiful." "There are probably aliens somewhere." "I'm thinking about having pizza for dinner." These all refer to things not in front of you right now.

Descriptive vs Prescriptive

The most important distinction in linguistics
›

Prescriptive grammar = rules telling you how you "should" speak ("don't split infinitives"). Descriptive grammar = describing how people actually speak. Linguistics is fundamentally descriptive.

Prescriptive view

"That's bad grammar!" / "Never end with a preposition"

Descriptive view

"That usage is common in informal registers and follows a pattern of notional agreement"

Example: "The committee has/have not decided." Prescriptivists argue about which is "correct," but linguists ask: when do speakers use each form, and why? (Answer: "have" when you're thinking of the group as individuals; "has" when thinking of it as a unit.)

💡
When you catch yourself thinking "that's wrong" about how someone speaks — stop. Ask instead: "Why do they say it that way? What pattern does it follow?" That's the linguistic mindset.
✎ Quick check
Is it "wrong" to say "they are not decided" instead of "they have not decided"? Explain using the descriptive/prescriptive distinction.
Not "wrong" — it's a different pattern. Prescriptively, it violates a rule. Descriptively, it shows that "decide" can be used as an adjective ("not decided") in some dialects or registers. Linguists describe what people say; they don't judge. If enough people say something, it's a valid part of the language.
7

Speech is Primary, Writing is Secondary

Separating sound from spelling
›

Every human community has spoken language. Not all have writing. Children learn to speak naturally without instruction; reading and writing must be taught. Writing was invented only ~5,000 years ago.

Naive view

English has 26 letters = 26 sounds

Linguistic reality

English has ~44 distinct sounds (phonemes) but only 26 letters; spelling often fails to capture pronunciation

English spelling is famously inconsistent: "ough" is pronounced differently in cough, through, though, tough, bough, thought. George Bernard Shaw's GHOTI = "fish" (GH as in 'enough', O as in 'women', TI as in 'nation').

✎ Quick check
The word "knight" has 6 letters. How many distinct sounds does it have when you say it aloud?
Three sounds: /n/ + /aɪ/ + /t/. The k, n, and gh are silent. This shows that letters ≠ sounds. Writing is just a visual code for speech; it often fails to represent the actual sounds.
8

The IPA: One Sound, One Symbol

A universal notation for speech
›

The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) solves the spelling problem by providing one symbol for each distinct sound in any human language. Created in 1888, continuously revised. The principle: bi-uniqueness — one symbol per sound, one sound per symbol.

The IPA chart organises:

  • Consonants by place of articulation (where: lips, teeth, palate) and manner (how: plosive, fricative, nasal)
  • Vowels by tongue height (high/mid/low) and backness (front/central/back)
💡
You don't need to memorise the entire IPA chart. Focus on understanding HOW it's organised — then you can always look up specific symbols. By Weeks 2–3, you'll be comfortable with the basics.
✎ Quick check
Why can't the English alphabet serve as a phonetic transcription system? Give two specific problems.
(1) One letter = many sounds: 'c' is /k/ in "cat" and /s/ in "city." (2) Many sounds = one letter: 'ough' sounds like /ɔf/, /u/, /oʊ/, /ʌf/ in different words. English spelling is historical, not phonetic. IPA solves this: one symbol = always one sound.
9

Language Endangerment

When languages die
›

Of 7,000 languages, most are endangered. There's a strong correlation between population size and endangerment: the smaller a language's speaker community, the more at risk it is.

Almost no Australian Aboriginal languages are spoken by more than 1,000 people. Some have only dozens of elderly speakers left. The key mechanism is intergenerational disruption — older speakers don't pass the language to children. Languages like English sit at the top-left (massive population, not endangered); languages like Kyadil sit at the bottom-right (tiny population, critically endangered).

⚠️
Why does this matter? When a language dies, humanity loses a unique way of expressing thought, unique knowledge encoded in that language (e.g., Aboriginal ecological knowledge), and a window into human cognition. Language diversity is cultural and intellectual heritage.
✎ Quick check
What is "intergenerational disruption" and why is it the key factor in language death?
Intergenerational disruption = when parents don't teach children the language. A language survives only if children learn it naturally from infants onward. If parents abandon the language (for economic reasons, discrimination, urbanisation), children don't acquire it, and it dies. It's the critical link in language transmission breaking.

Levels of Linguistic Analysis

The roadmap for the whole course
›

Linguistics analyses language at six levels, each building on the last:

Phonetics & Phonology (Weeks 2–5): How do we make and perceive sounds?
Morphology (Weeks 6–7): How do we build words from smaller parts?
Syntax (Weeks 8–9): How do we combine words into sentences?
Semantics & Pragmatics (Week 10+): How do we create and understand meaning?

✎ Quick check
Which level of analysis would you use to explain why "un-happy" is a valid English word but "happy-un" is not?
Morphology. "un-" is a prefix (goes before the root), and "-un" is not a standard suffix in English. This is a rule about word structure — how morphemes (meaningful units) can be combined. Phonetics/phonology only cares about sounds, not meaning. Syntax cares about sentences. Morphology is the right level.

Week 1 in one sentence

Linguistics is the scientific, descriptive study of how human language works — from the sounds we make to the complex social meanings we express. All 7,000 human languages are equally complex, speech is primary and writing secondary, and language is endangered but uniquely capable of infinite creativity through just a few levels of analysis.

📝 Week 1 Self-Test
10 exam-style questions. Try to answer each one before revealing. These cover the full study guide.
Q1Understanding

What is the approximate number of languages spoken in the world today?

Approximately 7,000 (estimates range from 6,000–8,000 depending on where you draw the line between "language" and "dialect"). Only about 4% have official status.
Q2Understanding

Name the four key design features of human language discussed in this week.

Arbitrariness (no natural link between form and meaning), displacement (talk about absent things), productivity (create novel sentences), and duality of patterning (meaningless units combine into meaningful ones).
Q3Understanding

Explain the difference between descriptive and prescriptive grammar.

Prescriptive grammar tells speakers how they "should" speak according to rules and standards. Descriptive grammar describes how speakers actually do speak, without judgment. Linguistics adopts the descriptive approach — we study what IS, not what "should be."
Q4Understanding

Why do linguists say "speech is primary and writing is secondary"?

All human communities have spoken language, but not all have writing. Children acquire speech naturally without instruction, while reading/writing must be taught. Writing was invented only ~5,000 years ago. Speech changes faster than writing, so writing often fails to reflect actual pronunciation.
Q5Understanding

What is the principle of bi-uniqueness in the IPA?

One symbol represents exactly one sound, and each sound is represented by exactly one symbol. This eliminates the ambiguity of conventional alphabets where one letter can represent multiple sounds (e.g., English 'c' = /k/ in 'cat' or /s/ in 'city').
Q6Apply

A friend says "Aboriginal languages are just simple dialects." Using what you learned this week, explain why this is wrong.

All human languages studied to date, including Aboriginal Australian languages, exhibit comparable complexity. Aboriginal languages aren't dialects of a single language — they are as different from each other as French is from Russian. The ~250+ distinct Australian Aboriginal languages each have complex grammar, phonology, and vocabulary. The "primitive language" myth was debunked by systematic scientific study.
Q7Apply

Give an original example of each of Hockett's four design features using English.

Arbitrariness: "chair" has no natural connection to the furniture. Displacement: "I'll meet you at the café tomorrow at 3pm" refers to a future time/place. Productivity: "My purple giraffe negotiated a ceasefire with the sentient toaster" — novel sentence never before uttered. Duality: /k/, /æ/, /t/ are meaningless individually but combine to form "cat".
Q8Apply

Analyse this sentence for all three language functions: "Did you hear? Sarah got the promotion — I'm so happy for her!"

Describing the world: reports the fact that Sarah got promoted. Organising language: "her" refers back to "Sarah" (anaphora). Maintaining social relationships: sharing good news, expressing solidarity, reinforcing the bond with the listener through genuine emotional support.
Q9Analyse

English has the same word "bank" for a financial institution and the side of a river. What does this tell us about the relationship between words and meanings?

This demonstrates arbitrariness — the link between a word's form and its meaning is not fixed or natural. If there were a natural connection between sound and meaning, one form couldn't have two unrelated meanings. This also shows that context, not the word itself, determines meaning.
Q10Analyse

Papua New Guinea has 860+ languages but only 0.1% of the world's population. Most of Europe, with far more people, has far fewer languages. What factors might explain this difference?

Historical: European languages underwent centuries of political consolidation (nation-states imposed single official languages). Geographic: PNG's mountainous terrain and island geography isolate communities. Social: proximity to the equator correlates with greater linguistic diversity. Political: colonialism and lingua francas reduced European diversity while PNG's decentralised political structure preserved it.
