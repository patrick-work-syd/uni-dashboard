Phonology: The Phoneme & Allophony

10 steps · Monday & Tuesday lectures · work through at your own pace

This week marks a big conceptual shift. You're moving from phonetics (physical sounds) to phonology (mental sound categories). The key question changes from "What sounds can humans make?" to "Which sound differences matter in a given language?" Take it step by step.

1

From Phonetics to Phonology

Physical sounds vs mental categories
›

Weeks 2 and 3 were about phonetics — the physical production, acoustics, and perception of speech sounds. This week we shift to phonology: how languages organise sounds into meaningful systems. Phonetics asks "What sounds exist?" Phonology asks "Which differences between sounds actually matter in this language?"

Phonetics (Weeks 2-3)

Studies the physical properties of speech sounds. Universal — applies to all languages the same way. Describes what humans CAN produce. Uses square brackets: [p], [pʰ].

Phonology (Week 4+)

Studies the mental organisation of sounds in a specific language. Language-specific — rules differ between languages. Describes what speakers KNOW. Uses slashes: /p/, /b/.

The central insight: speakers don't hear every physical difference between sounds. English speakers hear [pʰ] (aspirated) and [p] (unaspirated) as "the same sound" because the difference never changes meaning in English. But Hindi speakers hear them as completely different sounds. Phonology is about what your brain categorises as "same" vs "different."

Analogy

Think of handwriting. The letter "a" can be written in many different ways — tall, short, curly, printed, cursive. Physically they look different, but you recognise them all as the same letter. Phonology works the same way: physically different sounds get grouped into the same mental category.

✎ Quick check
What is the fundamental question that distinguishes phonology from phonetics?
Phonetics asks "What speech sounds can humans produce and how?" (physical/universal). Phonology asks "Which sound differences are meaningful in a particular language?" (mental/language-specific). Phonetics describes the raw material; phonology describes how each language organises that material into a system.
2

What Is a Phoneme?

Abstract category vs physical sound
›

A phoneme is an abstract mental category — a unit in the sound system of a language that can distinguish one word from another. A phone is any physical speech sound, regardless of its role in the system. Every phoneme is realised as one or more phones when actually spoken.

Phone (physical)

Written in square brackets: [p], [pʰ], [b]
Any speech sound actually produced.
Infinite variation — no two productions are identical.
"What you actually say."

Phoneme (abstract)

Written in slashes: /p/, /b/
A mental category that groups phones.
Finite set per language — English has ~44.
"What you think you're saying."

The notation is critical: /p/ means "the phoneme p" (abstract category), while [p] means "the phone p" (specific physical sound). When you see slashes, think "mental unit." When you see brackets, think "physical realisation."

💡
Slashes = mind, brackets = mouth. /p/ is what's in your head. [p] is what comes out of your mouth. A phoneme like /p/ may come out as [pʰ] in one context and [p] in another — same mental unit, different physical realisations.
✎ Quick check
If English has the phoneme /p/, and it can be pronounced as [pʰ] or [p] depending on context, how many phonemes and how many phones are we talking about?
One phoneme (/p/) and two phones ([pʰ] and [p]). The phoneme is the abstract category. The two phones are its physical realisations — called allophones. English speakers treat both as "the same sound" even though they are physically different.
3

Allophones: Variants of a Phoneme

Same category, different realisations
›

Allophones are the different phonetic variants of a single phoneme. They are phones that belong to the same mental category. Native speakers usually cannot hear the difference between allophones of the same phoneme — the variation is automatic and unconscious.

Example 1: English /p/

Say "pin" and hold your hand in front of your mouth. You feel a burst of air — that's aspiration. Now say "spin" — no air burst. The [pʰ] in "pin" and the [p] in "spin" are physically different, but English speakers hear both as /p/.

Example 2: English /l/

Say "lip" — the /l/ is clear [l] (tongue tip at alveolar ridge, front of tongue raised). Now say "pill" — the final /l/ is dark [ɫ] (tongue tip at alveolar ridge, but back of tongue raised toward velum). Same phoneme /l/, two allophones: [l] before vowels, [ɫ] after vowels or at word end.

⚠️
Common mistake: Don't confuse "allophone" with "phoneme." Allophones are the variants — they belong TO a phoneme. [pʰ] and [p] are allophones OF the phoneme /p/. They are not phonemes themselves.
✎ Quick check
Give the two allophones of English /l/ and state the environment where each appears.
Clear [l] appears before vowels (onset position), as in "lip" and "glow." Dark [ɫ] appears after vowels or at word end (coda position), as in "pill" and "milk." Both are allophones of the single phoneme /l/.
4

Minimal Pairs: The Test for Phonemes

Proving two sounds are separate phonemes
›

A minimal pair is two words that differ in exactly one sound and have different meanings. If you find a minimal pair, the two differing sounds MUST be separate phonemes — because swapping them changes the meaning.

More English minimal pairs: "bat" /bæt/ vs "pat" /pæt/ (proves /b/ ≠ /p/), "sip" /sɪp/ vs "zip" /zɪp/ (proves /s/ ≠ /z/), "bit" /bɪt/ vs "beat" /biːt/ (proves /ɪ/ ≠ /iː/).

💡
The logic: If swapping sound X for sound Y changes the word's meaning, then X and Y must be different phonemes. If it doesn't change meaning, they MIGHT be allophones (but you need more evidence — see Steps 5-7).
✎ Quick check
"Tip" and "dip" form a minimal pair. What does this prove, and why?
"Tip" /tɪp/ and "dip" /dɪp/ differ only in the initial consonant (/t/ vs /d/) and have different meanings. This proves that /t/ and /d/ are separate phonemes in English — the difference between them is contrastive (meaning-changing).
5

Complementary Distribution

Sounds that never compete for the same slot
›

Two sounds are in complementary distribution when they never appear in the same phonetic environment. Each one shows up only where the other doesn't — they fill complementary slots. This is the hallmark of allophones of the same phoneme.

The logic: if two sounds never appear in the same environment, you can never build a minimal pair with them. And if there's no minimal pair, they can't be separate phonemes. Instead, the choice between them is predictable from context — which is exactly what makes them allophones.

✅
Rule of thumb: Complementary distribution = allophones = same phoneme. The environment automatically determines which allophone appears. Speakers have no choice in the matter.
✎ Quick check
Why is it impossible to find a minimal pair for [pʰ] and [p] in English?
Because [pʰ] and [p] are in complementary distribution — they never occur in the same phonetic environment. [pʰ] only appears word-initially or in stressed onsets; [p] only appears after /s/ or in unstressed positions. Since a minimal pair requires two words that differ in the same position, and these sounds never compete for the same position, no minimal pair is possible.
6

Contrastive Distribution

Sounds that CAN appear in the same slot
›

Two sounds are in contrastive distribution when they CAN appear in the same environment AND swapping one for the other changes the meaning. This proves they are separate phonemes.

Contrastive (separate phonemes)

/p/ and /b/ in English:
"pin" /pɪn/ vs "bin" /bɪn/
"pat" /pæt/ vs "bat" /bæt/
Same environment, different meaning → minimal pair exists → separate phonemes.

Complementary (same phoneme)

[pʰ] and [p] in English:
"pin" [pʰɪn] — word-initial
"spin" [spɪn] — after /s/
Different environments → no minimal pair possible → allophones of /p/.

The decision tree is simple: Can these two sounds appear in the same environment? If YES and swapping changes meaning → contrastive distribution → separate phonemes. If NO (they never appear in the same position) → complementary distribution → allophones.

💡
Quick test: Can you find a minimal pair? If yes → separate phonemes (contrastive distribution). If no, and the sounds appear in mutually exclusive environments → allophones (complementary distribution).
✎ Quick check
Are /s/ and /z/ in contrastive or complementary distribution in English? Prove it.
Contrastive distribution. Minimal pairs: "sip" /sɪp/ vs "zip" /zɪp/, "bus" /bɐs/ vs "buzz" /bɐz/, "seal" /siːl/ vs "zeal" /ziːl/. They can appear in the same environments and swapping them changes meaning, so /s/ and /z/ are separate phonemes.
7

Free Variation

Same environment, no meaning change
›

There's a third possibility: two sounds appear in the same environment, but swapping one for the other does NOT change the meaning. This is called free variation. The sounds are interchangeable in that position — speakers can use either one freely.

Free variation tells us the two sounds are not contrastive (no meaning change), but they're also not in complementary distribution (they DO appear in the same environment). They may be allophones showing optional variation, or socially conditioned variants (e.g., dialectal differences).

⚠️
Exam trap: Don't confuse free variation with contrastive distribution. In both cases, the sounds appear in the same environment. The difference: contrastive = meaning changes; free variation = meaning stays the same.
✎ Quick check
If two sounds appear in the same environment but swapping them doesn't change meaning, what type of distribution is this? Are the sounds separate phonemes?
This is free variation. The sounds are NOT separate phonemes — since swapping them doesn't change meaning, they are not contrastive. They are typically allophones that happen to overlap in some environments, or optional variants. Unlike complementary distribution (where context determines the choice), in free variation the speaker can freely choose either sound.

9

Cross-Language Differences

Same sounds, different phonological status
›

The same pair of physical sounds can have completely different phonological status in different languages. This is one of the most important insights in phonology.

English: [pʰ] and [p] are ALLOPHONES

"pin" [pʰɪn] vs "spin" [spɪn] — same word meaning? No, but the words differ in more than just [pʰ]/[p]. No minimal pair for aspiration alone exists. These are allophones of /p/ in complementary distribution.

Hindi: [pʰ] and [p] are SEPARATE PHONEMES

[pʰal] "fruit" vs [pal] "moment" — minimal pair! Same environment, different meaning. Aspiration is contrastive in Hindi. /pʰ/ and /p/ are distinct phonemes.

This means an English speaker learning Hindi must learn to hear and produce a difference they've been ignoring their entire life. And a Hindi speaker learning English might over-distinguish sounds that English treats as the same.

💡
Why this matters: The physical sounds [pʰ] and [p] are available to all humans (phonetics is universal). But whether a language USES that difference to distinguish words is a language-specific phonological decision. Phonetics provides the palette; each language's phonology chooses which contrasts to paint with.

More examples: English /l/ and /r/ are separate phonemes ("light" vs "right"), but in Japanese they are allophones of a single phoneme. Korean has three-way voiceless stop contrasts (lax, tense, aspirated) where English has only two (voiced vs voiceless).

✎ Quick check
A Japanese speaker learning English struggles to distinguish "light" and "right." Using what you've learned, explain why.
In Japanese, [l] and [ɾ] (a tap similar to English [r]) are allophones of a single phoneme — they're in complementary distribution. Japanese speakers are trained to hear them as "the same sound." In English, /l/ and /r/ are separate phonemes (contrastive distribution: "light" vs "right"). The Japanese speaker's phonological system doesn't distinguish the two, making it hard to perceive and produce the English contrast.

Common mistake

Students often jump to conclusions without systematically listing environments first. Always start by cataloguing the data. Write down every word containing Sound A and note what comes before and after it. Do the same for Sound B. Only THEN compare the lists.

💡
Tutorial tip for Cree/Even/Japanese: Look for natural classes. If Sound A appears before front vowels and Sound B appears before back vowels, that's complementary distribution conditioned by vowel quality. The allophonic rule will reference the vowel environment.

Week 4 in one sentence

Phonology studies how languages organise physically different sounds (phones) into abstract mental categories (phonemes), using minimal pairs to prove contrast and complementary distribution to identify allophones — and the same physical sounds can have entirely different phonological status across languages. The method for any new data: list environments, check for overlap, then classify as contrastive, complementary, or free variation.

✎ Quick check
You're given data from an unfamiliar language and asked whether [s] and [ʃ] are allophones or separate phonemes. You find [s] only before [i, e] and [ʃ] only before [a, o, u]. What's your conclusion?
Complementary distribution — [s] appears only before front vowels [i, e] and [ʃ] appears only before non-front vowels [a, o, u]. Their environments never overlap. They are allophones of the same phoneme. The rule: the phoneme /s/ (or /ʃ/) → [s] before front vowels, [ʃ] elsewhere (or vice versa — to decide which is the "basic" form, choose the one with the less restricted distribution).
📝 Week 4 Self-Test
12 exam-style questions covering phonemes, allophones, and distribution. Try to answer each one before revealing.
Q1Remembering

What is the difference between a phoneme and a phone?

A phoneme is an abstract mental category — a unit in the sound system of a language that can distinguish meaning (written in slashes: /p/). A phone is any physical speech sound actually produced, regardless of its linguistic function (written in square brackets: [p]). A phoneme may be realised as multiple different phones (its allophones).
Q2Remembering

What notation is used for phonemes vs phones?

Phonemes are written between slashes: /p/, /b/, /t/. Phones are written between square brackets: [p], [pʰ], [b]. Slashes indicate an abstract, language-specific mental category. Square brackets indicate a concrete, physical sound.
Q3Understanding

What is a minimal pair and what does it prove?

A minimal pair is two words that differ in exactly one sound segment and have different meanings (e.g., "pin" /pɪn/ vs "bin" /bɪn/). A minimal pair proves that the two differing sounds are separate phonemes in that language, because the sound difference alone is enough to change the word's meaning — i.e., the contrast is phonemic.
Q4Understanding

Explain complementary distribution vs contrastive distribution.

Complementary distribution: two sounds never appear in the same phonetic environment — each occurs only where the other does not. This means they are allophones of the same phoneme (e.g., [pʰ] word-initially and [p] after /s/ in English). Contrastive distribution: two sounds CAN appear in the same environment, and swapping one for the other changes meaning. This means they are separate phonemes (e.g., /p/ and /b/ — "pin" vs "bin").
Q5Understanding

What is allophony?

Allophony is the phenomenon where a single phoneme has multiple phonetic realisations (allophones) that are predictable from context. The choice of allophone is automatic and rule-governed — determined by the surrounding sounds or position in the word. For example, English /p/ has the allophones [pʰ] (word-initial) and [p] (after /s/). Speakers produce the correct allophone unconsciously.
Q6Applying

Given "pin" [pʰɪn] and "spin" [spɪn], are [pʰ] and [p] separate phonemes or allophones? Explain your reasoning.

They are allophones of the phoneme /p/. Evidence: [pʰ] appears word-initially (before a vowel) and [p] appears after /s/. These are different environments — the two sounds are in complementary distribution. You cannot construct a minimal pair that differs only in [pʰ] vs [p], because they never occur in the same position. The aspiration is predictable from context, so it's allophonic, not phonemic.
Q7Applying

Write the phonological rule for English vowel nasalisation.

V → [Ṽ] / __[+nasal]. In prose: "A vowel becomes nasalised when it immediately precedes a nasal consonant (/m/, /n/, or /ŋ/)." Examples: "ban" [bæ̃n], "ham" [hæ̃m], "sing" [sɪ̃ŋ]. The tilde ( ̃) above the vowel indicates nasalisation. Oral and nasalised vowels are allophones in English — the nasalisation is predictable and never changes meaning.
Q8Applying

In Hindi, [pʰal] "fruit" and [pal] "moment" form a minimal pair. What does this tell us about the phonological status of [pʰ] and [p] in Hindi?

This minimal pair proves that [pʰ] and [p] are separate phonemes in Hindi (/pʰ/ and /p/). They appear in the same environment (word-initial, before the same vowel [a] and consonant [l]), and swapping one for the other changes the meaning. Aspiration is contrastive in Hindi, unlike English where it is allophonic. The same physical sounds have different phonological status in different languages.
Q9Applying

Describe the step-by-step method for analysing whether two sounds in an unfamiliar language are phonemes or allophones.

(1) List all words containing Sound A and note the environment (what precedes and follows it). (2) Do the same for Sound B. (3) Compare the environment lists: do the sounds ever appear in the same environment? (4a) If NO overlap → complementary distribution → allophones of one phoneme. Write the allophonic rule describing which allophone appears in which environment. (4b) If YES overlap → check whether swapping changes meaning. If meaning changes → contrastive distribution → separate phonemes. If no meaning change → free variation.
Q10Analysing

Why can the same physical sounds have different phonological status in different languages?

Because phonetics and phonology operate at different levels. Phonetics is universal — all humans have the same vocal apparatus and can produce the same range of sounds. But phonology is language-specific — each language independently decides which phonetic differences to use contrastively (to distinguish meaning) and which to ignore or treat as allophonic. There is no universal requirement that any particular phonetic distinction must be phonemic. The "choice" of which contrasts matter is an arbitrary, historical property of each language's sound system.
Q11Analysing

Given data from a language: [tasa] "sun", [muta] "moon", [sudu] "star", [diki] "sky", [teku] "cloud", [dumu] "rain" — are [t] and [d] allophones or separate phonemes? If allophones, write the rule.

List environments: [t] appears before [a, e] (tasa, muta, teku) — before low/mid vowels. [d] appears before [u, i] (sudu, diki, dumu) — before high vowels. The environments don't overlap → complementary distribution → allophones. Rule: /t/ → [d] / __V[+high] ("The phoneme /t/ is realised as [d] before high vowels [i, u], and as [t] elsewhere"). The voicing is predictable from the following vowel height.
Q12Analysing

How do Week 3's allophonic rules (aspiration, nasalisation, dark L) connect to Week 4's phoneme concept?

Week 3 introduced allophonic rules as phonetic processes (aspiration, nasalisation, dark L) without yet framing them in terms of phoneme theory. Week 4 provides the theoretical framework: these rules exist because the sounds involved are allophones of the same phoneme in complementary distribution. Aspiration ([pʰ] vs [p]) is allophonic because the sounds are in complementary distribution. Nasalisation ([æ] vs [æ̃]) is allophonic because it's predictable from the nasal consonant environment. Dark/clear L ([l] vs [ɫ]) is allophonic because position determines the variant. Week 3 described WHAT happens; Week 4 explains WHY — because languages organise sounds into phoneme categories with predictable variation.
